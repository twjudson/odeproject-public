If we let \(\alpha = 2\text{,}\) then there is a strong interaction between the two species and our system becomes
\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} & = x(1-x) - 2xy\\ \frac{dy}{dt} & = y(1-y) - 2xy. \end{align*}
We are interested in what happens as \(t \to \infty\text{.}\) First we will examine what happens when \(dx/dt =0\) and \(dy/dt =0\text{:}\)
\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} & = x(1 - x - 2y)\\ \frac{dy}{dt} & = y(1 - y - 2x). \end{align*}
If \(dx/dt = 0\text{,}\) then
\begin{align} x & = 0\tag{2.2.3}\\ y & = (1 - x)/2.\tag{2.2.4} \end{align}
Similarly, if \(dy/dt = 0\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align} y & = 0\tag{2.2.5}\\ y & = 1 - 2x.\tag{2.2.6} \end{align}
The lines in equations (2.2.3)â€“(2.2.6) are called nullclines. In general, if we are given the system
\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} & = f(x, y)\\ \frac{dy}{dt} & = g(x, y), \end{align*}
then the values of \(x\) and \(y\) satisfying \(dx/dt = f(x, y) = 0\) are called the \(x\)-nullclines of the system and the values of \(x\) and \(y\) satisfying the equation \(dy/dt = g(x, y) = 0\) are called the \(y\)-nullclines of the system.
